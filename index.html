Hello, cómo va, tudo bom? I’m a quantitative ecologist that uses models, math, and natural history to predict how the natural world behaves. My doctoral dissertation advances conceptual and theoretical frontiers in ecological science, specifically in our understanding of cooperation among species (mutualisms). My passion is telling stories through data, and I have strong creative thinking, public speaking, writing, teaching, and foreign language skills. I’m eager to continue applying data science and science-based methods to real-world challenges.

Experience

Research

Ecological Science
Doctoral dissertation, The University of Arizona: 2020 - 2024 (anticipated)

Quantitative researcher examining the effects of climate warming on species interactions, specifically mutualisms. The majority of my doctoral research involves the use of mathematical models and computations methods toward uncovering how thermal performance can be used to enhance our predictive abilities in determining when cooperation might persist or perish in the natural world. I've also conducted extensive preliminary lab work and fieldwork during my dissertation research, as well as particpated in and directed data science projects.

Social Science
Social Sciences Researcher, Stanford University: 2013 - 2016

Mixed-methods field researcher for the Osa & Golfito Initiative, an interdiscplinary conservation and development program in southern Costa Rica, at the Stanford Woods Institute for the Environment, Stanford University. In this role, I worked with diverse partners in the public and private sectors to develop community-based solutions to social and environmental challenges in the southern region. One of my major achievements from this work was co-creating a place- and community-based environmental education and leadership program (“SELAL”, 2014-16) for local youth that built from asset-based pedagogy. Apart from the program’s subsequent sustained regional social, economic, and environmental success, the resulting peer-reviewed publications (Cruz et al. 2018, Selby et al. 2020) were the first of its kind to demonstrate how community-based educational interventions can directly empower rural Latin American students and families in sustainable development decisions in the tropics.

Education
Research Assistant, The University of Arizona: 2018 - 2019

Qualitative researcher investigating the effects of research experience on pre- and in-service high school STEM teachers' teaching practices. This research was funded by the National Science Foundation, Robert Noyce Teacher Scholarship Program (Track 4) and approved by university IRB. During this research, I helped develop and implment interview protocols and methods, and evaluate interview data for emergent themes using validated coding techniques based on research questions. Our findings were synthesized by a novel framework that allowed enhanced predictive power in connecting student learning outcomes with teachers' prior research experiences and classroom teaching practices. These findings were presented at a national conference and documented in a manuscript.

Science Concepts and Practices in the Elementary and Middle School Curriculum (TLS 308)
Fall 2018: Dept. Teaching, Learning, & Sociocultural Studies, University of Arizona

Developed curricula for new course designed for students who are preparing to become elementary and middle school teachers and students interested in working in elementary and middle schools. Course introduces students to the big ideas in the elementary and middle school science curriculum in the areas of physical science, life science, Earth and space science, and engineering. Students explore these ideas by engaging in scientific and engineering practices such as asking questions, conducting investigations, constructing explanations, and designing solutions. The course also provides an introduction to the nature of science as well as the power and limitations of science as an enterprise. Students will become familiar with state and national science education standards and common ways children make sense of science ideas.

Sustainability & Education (TLS 200)
Spring 2018: Dept. Teaching, Learning, & Sociocultural Studies, University of Arizona

This course explored the complex web of relationships between environmental, economic, and social systems. In it students studied the causes, consequences, and alternatives related to the exploitation of natural resources, the distribution of wealth, the consumption of goods and services in society, and its relationship to formal and non-formal education.

Study of U.S. Institutes Program
Spring 2018: Dept. Latin American Studies, University of Arizona

Facilitated academic experiences for visiting students from Peru, Bolivia, Ecuador, and Paraguay. Program funded by the U.S. State Department. Students took classes from UA faculty and community experts on feminism and women’s movements in U.S. history, social entrepreneurship, Native American Nations, immigration and border policy, democracy, and environment and sustainability. Students participated in leadership workshops, volunteered for community service projects, visited the Grand Canyon, and received hands-on training in sustainable technologies as well as video making. The institute was conducted entirely in Spanish.

Schooling & Diversity: Race, class, language(s), LGBT & immigration (TLS 150C1)
Fall 2018: Dept. Teaching, Learning, & Sociocultural Studies, University of Arizona

This course provided students a broad exposure to systemic (K-12) schooling inequities experienced by diverse groups of non-mainstream students. Students read/viewed, critically analyzed, and applied a variety of multimodal sources (e.g., films, documentaries, video clips, scholarly work, magazine articles, charts, and reputable institutional websites). The course presented issues of power and privilege and the ways in which socially constructed factors, such as race, ethnicity, class (socio-economic status), sexual identities, and other socially constructed factors, interact with educational (mis)opportunities and (under)achievement. The course also provided opportunities for students to analyze their own educational experiences from a critical perspective. Course goals included: 1) develop students' critical and quantitative literacy skills (through the integration of quantitative charts on course topics), and 2) make connections between various historical events and present-day educational issues and concerns.

Conservation and Development Dilemmas in the Amazon (HUMBIO 18SC/ANTHRO 11SC)
Summer 2013: Sophomore College, Stanford University

An 11-day experiential field course for Stanford students exploring the social and environmental challenges that diverse communities face in Peru and the Peruvian Amazon. Students developed, gathered data, and reported on their field projects through the duration of the trip. Student group traveled with Stanford Alumni, and class was directed by Faculty Leaders Dr. William Durham and Julia Novy-Hildesley.
